It is the price of luxury insurance in a market where the term has become a contradiction. The doctors still drop her.

If you are paying thirty thousand dollars a year for an insurance card and you still cannot see your physician, the natural psychological reaction is to assume you are at fault. We must be using the system incorrectly. We must have bought the wrong plan.

American healthcare has bifurcated into two markets. A two-partner LLC in New York at sub-$50k revenue does not have a single clean legal pathway from the individual wasteland into the commercial market. There are four legitimate routes, one contested gray zone, and a great deal of broker folklore. This paper maps each — and the regulatory wall that makes the map necessary.

I.
Chapter the First · The Diagnosis

A market that stopped working.

To guarantee coverage for his family, Jesse and Michelle did the dutiful thing: they purchased the most expensive tier available on the NY State of Health marketplace.1 Their reasoning was the reasoning of careful people. If a Platinum plan does not work, surely nothing does.

The financial burden is staggering. Yet Michelle's private-practice physicians continuously drop her. One office, then another, then another. The card remains. The access does not.

This is not a defect. It is the design.

The individual marketplace operates under guaranteed-issue rules.2 Carriers must accept all comers regardless of pre-existing condition. In a market without a meaningful federal subsidy floor, the result is adverse selection: the highest-risk patients concentrate onto the individual ledger. To protect their margins, carriers do two things — they narrow networks, and they cut physician reimbursement.3 Private-practice doctors, reading the math, simply decline the carrier contracts. Documented

The state exchange becomes a containment vessel. Patients pay luxury prices for a card no reputable doctor wants to swipe. You are not utilizing the system incorrectly. The system is utilizing you correctly.

You are not dumb. The system is structurally cruel. — A finding of fact.

Healthcare in 2026 has, quietly and without announcement, violently bifurcated.

The exchange is not a safety net — it is a containment vessel.

To save his wife from the wasteland, Jesse needs to use Unsubscribe NYC LLC to walk across the border into the commercial market. The LLC is not a tax dodge. It is a passport. But the passport requires a stamp the State of New York has quietly stopped issuing to entities like his.

II.
Chapter the Second · The Eligibility Wall

A definition with teeth.

To cross from the individual market into the commercial small group market, an entity needs more than a desire to. It needs to qualify — legally, definitionally — as a small employer. The definition has quiet teeth.

Under the Affordable Care Act, a "small employer" is one that employs at least one but no more than fifty employees on average during the preceding calendar year.4 The crucial word is employee. Federal regulation defines the term using the IRS common-law test — the same test that distinguishes a W-2 employee from a 1099 contractor — which expressly excludes business owners, partners in a partnership, and self-employed individuals from the count.5

Affordable Care Act · §1304(b) · Definition of "Small Employer"

"… an employer who employed an average of at least 1 but not more than 100 employees on business days during the preceding calendar year … "

NY adopted federal definition · effective 1 Jan 20166 Verified

New York adopted the federal definition for its fully-insured small-group market effective January 1, 2016.6 The practical consequence is severe and almost never advertised: a New York LLC consisting of two partners and no other workers has, in the eyes of the small group market, zero employees. It is not a small employer. It is not eligible.

This is the wall. It is short, quietly built, and almost no one mentions it because the business of small group insurance depends on routing around it. Underwriters at Oxford and Aetna will, when pressed, demand evidence of at least one common-law employee — usually through the NYS-45 quarterly wage filing,7 a document a founders-only LLC has nothing to submit for.

The original premise — "just file two K-1s and apply for Oxford Freedom" — is a broker's pitch, not a regulator's blessing. Contested Sometimes it works. It is not how the rule is written. The honest map requires acknowledging which routes are inside the rule, and which are routes around it.

III.
Chapter the Third · The Menu

Six routes, ranked by candor.

What follows is the audit. Five legitimate routes exist for a two-partner LLC in New York seeking commercial-market access. A sixth, the K-1 gambit, is the gray-zone workaround the rest of this paper grapples with directly. Each is named, costed, and tagged with its current regulatory status.

IV.
Chapter the Fourth · The PEO Cliff

When the legal route becomes the cliff.

Of the five legitimate pathways, the PEO is the most legible — and the most punishing for a sub-$50k LLC. The PEO solves the eligibility problem cleanly: founders become W-2 employees of the PEO, the PEO is the small employer, the math is unimpeachable. The trouble is the math.

V.
Chapter the Fifth · The K-1 Gambit

A workaround, described honestly.

The K-1 gambit deserves its own chapter — not because it is the cleanest answer, but because it is the answer most brokers will pitch first, and most founders will hear about first. To leave it out would be to leave the most-traveled path off the map.

The thesis of the gambit is that a sufficiently legitimate-looking partnership — equal partners, capital contributions documented, ordinary business income filed — can sometimes obtain fully-insured small group coverage in New York despite having no common-law employees. The mechanism is paperwork. The IT-204-IP becomes the underwriter's evidence that the entity is a real business taking real risk, and not a shell organized at the post office to grab a card.

It sometimes works. Brokers who specialize in professional partnerships — creative agencies, law firms, design studios, software shops — have routed founder-only LLCs through specific carriers and association programs.16 But this is not regulatory blessing. It is enforcement variance. The same application that clears one underwriter on a Monday can be returned by another on a Tuesday.

What an honest application looks like, on paper:

To be clear about what the gambit is and is not: it is enforcement variance, not a legal carve-out. The cleanest version of the K-1 route is usually a K-1 route plus an AHP — the partnership joins an association whose plan is more permissive about owner-only groups, and the IT-204-IP becomes the credentialing document. Pursued naked, without an association or a specialist broker, the K-1 application is most likely to be returned with a request for an NYS-45.7

Coda · §

The honest answer.

There is no clean exit. The Eligibility Wall stands, and the State of New York is not going to lower it for a two-partner LLC at sub-$50k revenue. The marketplace is what it is. The doctors are doing what doctors do. The Affordable Care Act, as currently interpreted, has quietly closed the front door on owner-only partnerships and left a series of side doors of varying legitimacy.

Jesse's realistic playbook, in order of cleanliness, is some combination of the following:

  1. Consult a NY-licensed insurance broker who specializes in small partnerships — not the broker on the marketplace website. Specialists know which carriers currently accept which structures and which AHPs are still actively writing.
  2. Consider hiring one part-time common-law employee — bookkeeper, ops assistant, contractor-converted-to-W-2. Even at 15–20 hr/wk and ~$25k loaded, this is often the cheapest and most legally durable unlock. The economics work as soon as the LLC clears ~$60–75k in revenue.
  3. Investigate AHPs in adjacent industries — creative, software, professional services, freelance unions. Membership is usually $200–$500/yr and unlocks group plans with looser owner-only rules.
  4. Document everything. Partnership agreement. Capital contributions. Operating bank statements. Contracts and invoices. Whatever route is taken, paper trails are what decide outcomes when an underwriter audits.
  5. Hold the self-employed deduction as a backstop. If every other route fails this year, deduct premiums on Schedule 1 and re-attempt next year with cleaner books.

The K-1 gambit is not the hero of this story. The hero, if there is one, is the honest broker — and the persistence of a family who refuses to accept that the wasteland is the only place left to live.
